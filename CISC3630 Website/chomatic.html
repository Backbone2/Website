Notice how it repeats?

Looking at only the white keys, you will notice that there is a distict pattern. The keys go from A - G and then repeat.

You also will notice that the black keys have two names, each corresponding to the two white keys that surround it. These are known as sharp (♯) and flat (♭). Depending on what key a song is in will determine which name is used. However, they are exactly the same note! For example, D♭ and C♯ are completely interchangable. As are E♭ and D♯, G♭ and F♯ etc.

Semitones

Each key representes 1 semitone. There are 12 semitones in the chromatic scale. Starting from the left of the above picture C, the next semitone is the black key (D♭/C♯). The next semitone is the white key D. This pattern continues until you reach the B. If you count, you can see that there are exactly 12 semitones (keys) from the C to the B.

Quick quiz:

If you start at the note D and you move up the scale 5 semitones, what note are you at?

The answer is: G!

Starting from D counting up 1 semitone at a time: D♯, E, F, G♭, G. Likewise, 5 semitones down from G is D. Don't forget! Black keys have two names that are interchangable. E♭ and D♯ are the exact same note. As are G♭ and F♯.

Chords

A Chord, in music, is any harmonic set of three or more notes played simontaneously or in some semblance of succession. Chords are the structure on which songs are built. The combination of certain semitones (notes) of the Chromatic scale form chords. The few different types and combinations can be found and referenced here.
